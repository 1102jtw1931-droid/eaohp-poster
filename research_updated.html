How recovery experiences shape nurses' perceptions of workplace mistreatment

A multilevel daily diary study examining how accumulated recovery experiences build psychological resources that protect nurses from perceiving negative workplace interactions as mistreatment.

Authors Taeuk Jeon, Seonmi Yoon, Sun-ha Kim, Seohyun Ji, Sunhee Lee

Method Multilevel Structural Equation Modeling

Background

Why recovery experiences matter

Workplace mistreatment is a prevalent source of employee strain; however, such behaviors are not always clear-cut and often require active interpretation.

Employees' psychological resources can influence how they interpret workplace interactions, enabling more objective appraisal of ambiguous behaviors and less threat perception.

However, psychological resources are finite and susceptible to depletion, making effective recovery essential for their replenishment.

The present study tests whether recovery experiences enhance recovery state, which in turn reduces perceptions of workplace mistreatment at both the daily and person levels.

Method

Experience sampling over one month

Participants: 268 registered nurses employed at hospitals in South Korea.

Procedure: Using experience sampling methodology, two surveys per workday (an after-work survey and a before-work survey) were conducted over one month. A total of 3,917 daily observations were obtained (M = 14.6 days per person).

Measures: Recovery experiences were assessed across four dimensions: psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control (4 items). Recovery state (2 items). Workplace mistreatment perception (10 items): patients/caregivers (4 items), supervisor (3 items), coworker (3 items).

Analysis: Multilevel structural equation modeling using Mplus 8.9.

Results

Person-level effects drive the finding

Recovery experiences significantly predicted recovery state at both the daily and person levels. At the daily level, recovery experiences directly and negatively predicted mistreatment perception, independent of recovery state — neither the effect of recovery state nor the indirect effect was significant. At the person level, recovery state significantly and negatively predicted mistreatment perception, and the indirect effect of recovery experiences through recovery state was significant.

Discussion

What this means for nurses and organizations

Recovery experience as a predictor of workplace mistreatment perception: Recovery experiences predicted mistreatment perception at both levels, suggesting that how well employees recover from work extends beyond well-being outcomes to shape how they interpret interpersonal workplace events. This reframes mistreatment perception as driven by a modifiable recovery state rather than fixed dispositional traits (e.g., trait NA).

Level-dependent mechanisms: At the daily level, recovery experiences reduced mistreatment perception directly. At the person level, the effect operated solely through accumulated recovery state. The divergent patterns across levels call for a temporally differentiated model of how recovery shapes mistreatment perception, with daily and chronic processes operating through distinct psychological pathways.

Future research questions: Why do daily and person-level effects operate through different pathways? Does recovery enhance perceptual accuracy, or does it merely attenuate mistreatment perception? Is perceiving less mistreatment adaptive reappraisal or desensitization to genuine mistreatment?
