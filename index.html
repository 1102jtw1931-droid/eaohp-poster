How recovery experiences shape nurses' perceptions of workplace mistreatment

A multilevel daily diary study examining how accumulated recovery experiences build psychological resources that protect nurses from perceiving negative workplace interactions as mistreatment.

Authors Taeuk Jeon, Seonmi Yoon, Sun-ha Kim, Seohyun Ji, Sunhee Lee
Affiliation Department of Psychology, Chungnam National University
Method Multilevel Structural Equation Modeling

"The accumulation of recovery experiences builds psychological resources that protect nurses from perceiving negative workplace interactions as mistreatment."

Background

Why recovery experiences matter

Workplace mistreatment is a prevalent interpersonal stressor and a significant source of strain for employees. Employees' psychological resources influence how they perceive and interpret potentially negative workplace interactions.

Sufficient psychological resources enable employees to appraise ambiguous interpersonal behaviors more objectively and feel less threatened. Effective recovery is a key mechanism for replenishing these psychological resources.

Method

Experience sampling over one month

Using experience sampling methodology, daily surveys were conducted on each workday over one month — consisting of a post-work survey and a next-morning survey. Recovery experiences were assessed across four dimensions: psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control. Workplace mistreatment was measured across three sources: patients/caregivers (4 items), supervisors (3 items), and coworkers (3 items).

Data were analyzed using multilevel structural equation modeling (MSEM) in Mplus 8.9.

Results

Person-level effects drive the finding

Recovery experiences significantly predicted recovery state at both levels. However, the indirect path to mistreatment was only significant at the between (person) level — suggesting it is the accumulation of recovery over time, not daily fluctuations, that shapes mistreatment perceptions.

Discussion

What this means for nurses and organizations

At the person level, recovery experiences reduced perceived workplace mistreatment through recovery state, whereas this indirect effect was not significant at the daily level.

This suggests that daily fluctuations in recovery may not immediately influence perceptions of workplace mistreatment; rather, it is the accumulation of recovery experiences over time that shapes how employees perceive negative workplace interactions.

These findings highlight the importance of fostering a work environment that consistently supports nurses' recovery to reduce their perceptions of workplace mistreatment.
